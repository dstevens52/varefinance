<QuickAnswer>
**Quick Answer:** VA loans are assumable, meaning a qualified buyer can take over the seller's existing VA loan — including the interest rate — rather than originating a new loan at current market rates. The buyer must qualify with the current loan servicer through a credit and income review. The buyer does not need to be a veteran. If a non-veteran assumes the loan, the selling veteran's entitlement stays tied to that loan until it's paid off — a significant consideration for veterans planning to use their benefit again. The selling veteran should always request a formal release of liability to avoid remaining on the hook if the buyer defaults. The VA charges a 0.5% assumption fee.
</QuickAnswer>

## What Makes VA Loans Assumable

Most conventional mortgages contain a due-on-sale clause that requires the full loan balance to be paid at the time of transfer. VA loans do not. The VA has always permitted qualified buyers to assume an existing VA loan, taking over the balance, rate, and remaining term in place of the original borrower.

This is one of the oldest and most underappreciated features of the VA loan program. For most of the past decade, it attracted little attention — when new mortgages were available at 3–4%, there was no compelling reason to pursue an assumption. In 2026, with 30-year mortgage rates sitting well above 6%, the math has changed dramatically. A loan originated at 3.5% in 2021 is worth real money to a buyer.

## Who Can Assume a VA Loan?

**Anyone can assume a VA loan — the buyer does not need to be a veteran.**

This surprises many people, but it is accurate. The assumability feature runs with the loan, not with the buyer's military status. A civilian buyer with no military service can assume a veteran's VA loan, take over the existing rate, and make payments going forward exactly as the original borrower would have.

This creates a significant selling advantage for veterans in a high-rate environment. A VA loan at 3.5% is an asset that any buyer can access — not just veterans. That broadens the pool of interested buyers and can support a higher sale price.

That said, the buyer must qualify. Assumption is not a back door around lending standards.

## The Qualification Process

Assuming a VA loan is not automatic or paperwork-free. The buyer must go through a qualification process with the current loan servicer — the company that receives the monthly payments on the existing loan.

The servicer will review:

- **Credit history** — the buyer's credit score and payment history
- **Income and employment** — the buyer must demonstrate ability to repay the loan
- **Debt-to-income ratio** — the servicer applies standard DTI analysis

The VA also reviews and approves the assumption for loans still in active VA servicing. For loans that have been sold out of VA servicing to a private investor, the lender's own approval standards govern.

The process is handled entirely through the servicer, not through a new lender. The buyer does not go loan shopping — they work with the existing loan's servicer from start to finish. Plan for **45 to 90 days** from application to assumption completion. This is meaningfully longer than some buyers expect, and the timeline should factor into purchase contract negotiations.

## What Happens to the Veteran's Entitlement?

This is the most consequential question for the selling veteran, and it hinges on one factor: whether the buyer is an eligible veteran or a non-veteran.

### If a Non-Veteran Assumes the Loan

When a civilian buyer assumes a VA loan, the **selling veteran's entitlement remains tied to that loan** until the balance is paid off in full. The entitlement does not transfer to the buyer — there is no entitlement to transfer. The VA simply continues to guarantee the existing loan.

The practical consequence: the selling veteran may not be able to use their full VA loan entitlement on a future purchase until the assumed loan is paid off. Depending on the loan balance and how much bonus entitlement the veteran has available, this may or may not prevent them from using a VA loan on their next home. Veterans with remaining entitlement can sometimes still use the benefit, but the calculation is more complicated.

Veterans who are certain they will want to use a VA loan again in the near future should think carefully before allowing a non-veteran to assume their loan without understanding this trade-off. See our guide to [VA loan entitlement restoration](/blog/va-loan-entitlement-restoration) for a full explanation of how entitlement works and what partial entitlement means in practice.

### If an Eligible Veteran Assumes the Loan

When a qualified veteran assumes the loan, the selling veteran has an option: they can request **entitlement substitution**. In this process, the assuming veteran's entitlement takes the place of the selling veteran's entitlement on the existing loan — effectively freeing the seller's entitlement even though the loan remains outstanding.

Entitlement substitution requires VA approval and formal paperwork. Both veterans must be eligible, and the assuming veteran must have sufficient entitlement to substitute. If approved, the selling veteran's entitlement is released and available for future use.

This is the cleanest outcome for a selling veteran who wants to use the VA loan benefit again. Entitlement substitution is less common than a standard assumption but is a legitimate and viable path when the buyer is also a veteran.

## Release of Liability: A Critical Step Sellers Often Miss

Even when an assumption closes cleanly, the selling veteran can remain legally obligated on the loan after the buyer takes over — unless they formally request a **release of liability**.

Without a release of liability, the VA and the lender can pursue the original borrower if the assuming buyer defaults. The loan remains on the veteran's credit report, and a default by the new buyer can damage the veteran's credit and financial standing even though the veteran no longer lives in the home or makes payments.

Requesting a release of liability is not automatic — the seller must ask for it explicitly, and the VA must approve it. The servicer initiates the process as part of the assumption. Approval depends on the buyer's creditworthiness and the servicer's review.

**Always request a release of liability.** A clean assumption without a release creates ongoing financial risk for the selling veteran that most sellers don't fully appreciate until something goes wrong.

## The 0.5% Assumption Fee

The VA charges a 0.5% assumption fee on the loan balance at the time of assumption. This is the same percentage as the IRRRL funding fee. On a $250,000 remaining balance, that's $1,250.

The fee is paid by the assuming buyer, not the seller, unless negotiated otherwise in the purchase contract. It can be paid at closing or, in some cases, rolled into the assumed balance depending on the servicer's allowances.

Compared to the costs of originating a new mortgage — origination fees, points, title work, and a much higher rate — the 0.5% assumption fee is typically minor.

## The Equity Gap Problem

The most common practical barrier to VA loan assumptions is the equity gap.

Consider a home worth $420,000 with an existing VA loan balance of $275,000. The seller's equity is $145,000. A buyer who assumes the loan takes over the $275,000 balance — but the seller still needs to be made whole on their equity. That means the buyer must come up with $145,000 beyond the loan assumption.

Options for covering the gap:
- **Cash** — the buyer brings the equity gap to closing as cash
- **Second mortgage** — the buyer takes out a separate loan to cover the difference
- **Seller financing** — the seller carries a note for part of the gap (less common, requires negotiation)

VA loans cannot be used to cover this gap — the VA does not permit a second VA loan behind an assumed VA loan. Conventional second mortgages are possible but add complexity and cost.

In a hot market with limited inventory, buyers who are motivated and can cover the equity gap find VA assumption a compelling option. The obstacle is that many buyers who would benefit from a low-rate assumption cannot easily access $100,000 or more in cash or secondary financing. This limits the practical pool of assumption buyers to those with meaningful liquidity or high incomes who can qualify for a second mortgage.

## Why This Matters in 2026

Veterans who purchased homes in 2020 and 2021 locked in rates between 2.5% and 3.75% — some of the lowest mortgage rates in American history. Those loans are still outstanding on homes that have appreciated significantly.

A buyer who can assume a 3.0% VA loan on a $300,000 balance instead of originating a new loan at 6.5% saves roughly $700 per month in principal and interest. Over five years, that's $42,000 in savings before considering the compounding benefit of paying down a lower balance faster.

That spread is large enough to change what buyers can afford and what sellers can ask. Veterans sitting on these loans hold a genuine advantage in the current market — one that extends to any buyer, not just other veterans.

For veterans who locked in at high 2023–2024 rates and are now considering refinancing, the assumption feature works the other direction: [understanding whether to refinance now or wait](/blog/when-to-refinance-va-loan) is its own decision, separate from whether to allow an assumption. Both options exist, and the right path depends on your specific rate, balance, and plans.

## How the Assumption Process Works Step by Step

1. **Buyer and seller agree to the assumption** in the purchase contract, with clear language about who covers the assumption fee and how the equity gap is handled
2. **Buyer contacts the current servicer** and requests an assumption package
3. **Buyer submits a credit and income application** through the servicer
4. **VA reviews and approves** (for loans still in VA servicing)
5. **Servicer and VA complete paperwork**, including release of liability if requested
6. **Assumption closes** — title transfers to the buyer, loan remains in place
7. **Entitlement substitution is processed** (if the buyer is an eligible veteran and the seller requests it)

The paperwork-intensive nature of this process is why the 45–90 day timeline is realistic. Veterans and buyers who try to rush assumptions into a 30-day close window frequently run into problems.

## Bottom Line

VA loan assumption is a meaningful feature that becomes especially valuable in high-rate environments. For selling veterans with low-rate loans, it can attract buyers and support higher prices. For buying veterans or civilians, assuming a 3–4% loan instead of originating at 6%+ produces real financial benefit.

The non-negotiable items for sellers: always request a release of liability, understand what happens to your entitlement, and factor in the equity gap when pricing the transaction. For buyers: be prepared for a longer timeline than a conventional purchase, have a plan for covering the equity gap, and confirm you can qualify through the servicer.
