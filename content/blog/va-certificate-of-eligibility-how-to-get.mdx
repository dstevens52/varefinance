## What the COE Is — and Why It Matters

The Certificate of Eligibility is the VA's official confirmation that you are eligible to use your VA home loan benefit. It is required for every VA loan transaction: a purchase loan, a [VA IRRRL](/va-irrrl) (streamline refinance), or a [VA cash-out refinance](/va-cash-out).

The COE does not approve you for a loan or guarantee a particular rate. It is a prerequisite — a document your lender must have on file before VA backing can be applied to your mortgage. Without it, no VA loan can close.

The COE contains three key pieces of information:

- **Your entitlement amount** — the dollar amount the VA will guarantee on your behalf (currently $36,000 base entitlement, with bonus entitlement available in higher-cost areas)
- **Prior VA loan usage** — whether you have outstanding VA-backed loans that have tied up some entitlement
- **Entitlement status** — whether your full entitlement is available, partially available, or needs to be restored

Understanding what the COE shows helps you recognize potential issues before they slow down your closing.

## Method 1: Through Your Lender (Fastest)

For most veterans, the simplest path is to do nothing and let the lender handle it.

VA-approved lenders have electronic access to the VA's Web LGY (Loan Guaranty) system. When you apply for a VA loan, your lender can submit a COE request directly into that system and typically receive a response within minutes. No paperwork from you, no waiting, no forms to complete.

This is the most common path by a wide margin — the majority of COEs issued today are pulled electronically by lenders, not requested by veterans independently.

**When lender-pull works best:** You have a straightforward service history (active duty or honorably discharged veteran with no complex situations), your prior VA loans are visible in the system, and you're working with a lender experienced in VA loans.

**When it may not work:** If your service records are not yet in the VA's database — common for recently separated veterans — the system may not find your record, and you may need to provide documentation directly.

## Method 2: Through VA.gov (Self-Service)

If you want to obtain your COE independently before approaching a lender, VA.gov offers a self-service portal where you can request and often immediately download it.

Go to the <a href="https://www.va.gov/housing-assistance/home-loans/how-to-request-coe/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">VA's COE request page</a> at VA.gov. You'll need a verified VA.gov account (you can log in via Login.gov or ID.me). Once logged in, the system pulls your service record and, if it finds a match, generates your COE on the spot as a downloadable PDF.

This path is useful if:
- You want to review your COE and entitlement status before talking to a lender
- You are shopping lenders and want to hand them your COE rather than wait for each one to pull it
- Your situation is straightforward and your service record is in the VA's system

If the online system can't automatically generate your COE — which happens when the required service record data isn't available electronically — it will prompt you to submit documentation. In that case, follow Method 3 below.

## Method 3: By Mail Using VA Form 26-1880 (Slowest)

The original method of obtaining a COE is to mail a completed VA Form 26-1880 to the VA with supporting documentation. The VA processes these requests in the order received.

Processing time: **four to six weeks**. In some cases longer during high-volume periods.

There is essentially no scenario where this is the preferred path if either of the electronic options works for you. It exists as a fallback for veterans whose records are not in electronic systems — primarily veterans who served before full electronic record-keeping was standard — or as a backup when system issues prevent electronic processing.

If you must use the mail method, send your completed form to:
**VA Eligibility Center, P.O. Box 20729, Winston-Salem, NC 27120**

## What Documents You Need

The documents required depend on your service status:

### Discharged Veterans
The key document is your **DD-214** (Certificate of Release or Discharge from Active Duty). The DD-214 shows your discharge date, character of discharge, and service details the VA uses to verify eligibility.

If your discharge was honorable or under honorable conditions, you are generally eligible. Other than honorable (OTH) discharges create eligibility questions that vary by case — the VA evaluates these individually.

### Active Duty Service Members
Active duty members cannot provide a DD-214 (they haven't been discharged). Instead, you need a **statement of service** signed by your commanding officer, adjutant, or personnel officer. It must include your full name, Social Security number, date of birth, date entered active duty, duration of lost time (if any), and the name of the commanding officer.

### National Guard and Reserve Members
Eligibility for National Guard and Reserve members depends on whether you were activated under federal orders. If so:
- **Activated under Title 10 (federal orders):** DD-214 from that activation
- **National Guard members:** NGB Form 22 (National Guard Report of Separation) plus your retirement points statement
- **Reserve members:** Your retirement points statement and proof of honorable service

Guard and Reserve eligibility rules are more complex than active duty or veteran rules. If you're unsure whether your service qualifies, your lender or a VA regional loan center can help assess your situation.

### Surviving Spouses
Surviving spouses of veterans who died in service or from a service-connected disability may be eligible for VA loan benefits. Required documentation includes the veteran's DD-214, your marriage certificate, and the veteran's death certificate. If you receive Dependency and Indemnity Compensation (DIC), provide documentation of that as well.

## What the COE Shows — and Common Issues

### Prior VA Loan Usage

If you've used a VA loan before, your COE will show entitlement that has been tied up. This doesn't necessarily prevent you from getting another VA loan — it depends on whether that entitlement has been restored or whether you have remaining bonus entitlement available.

If your prior VA loan has been paid off and the property sold, your entitlement is eligible for restoration — but the VA doesn't restore it automatically. You need to request restoration. Our guide to [VA loan entitlement restoration](/blog/va-loan-entitlement-restoration) covers the process in detail.

### Lost or Missing DD-214

If you can't locate your DD-214, you can request a copy from the **National Archives** through their eVetRecs online system at archives.gov/veterans/military-service-records. Standard requests can take several weeks; some requests with documentation of an urgent need (such as a pending home purchase) are expedited.

You can also request a DD-214 through your state's Department of Veterans Affairs or through the National Personnel Records Center in St. Louis.

Don't let a missing DD-214 delay a purchase. Contact your lender early — they may be able to begin processing while you wait for the replacement, and the lender pull through Web LGY may work without it if your record is already in the system.

### Entitlement Partially Used

If you have an active VA loan on another property, your entitlement is partially tied up. You may still have enough remaining entitlement to buy another property (depending on loan size and county loan limits). Your COE will show exactly how much entitlement is available. Your lender can calculate whether it's sufficient for the loan you need.

## IRRRL: A Special Case

If you're doing a VA IRRRL (streamline refinance) rather than a purchase or cash-out, the COE process is even simpler.

Because the IRRRL is a refinance of an existing VA loan, the VA loan is already on file in the system. Your lender can verify your eligibility through the existing loan record — there is typically no need for you to independently obtain or provide a COE. The lender will pull it electronically as part of normal processing, and the fact that you have an active VA loan on the property is itself sufficient confirmation of prior eligibility.

This is one of the reasons the [VA IRRRL](/va-irrrl) is so streamlined — most of the eligibility verification that would otherwise require fresh documentation is handled automatically through the existing loan record. See our full breakdown of [VA IRRRL documentation requirements](/blog/va-irrrl-documents-needed) for a complete picture of what's required.

## Practical Tips

**Apply with a VA-experienced lender.** Lenders who originate high volumes of VA loans process COE requests regularly and know exactly how to handle edge cases — missing records, Guard and Reserve eligibility, entitlement restoration. A lender who rarely does VA loans may be less familiar with these nuances.

**Check your COE before you need it.** If you're thinking about a VA loan in the next six to twelve months, consider pulling your COE now through VA.gov. You'll see your current entitlement status, identify any issues, and have time to resolve them without a closing date creating pressure.

**Don't assume problems are unfixable.** Common COE issues — missing records, prior usage, partial entitlement — have standard resolution paths. A lender or VA regional loan center can walk you through them.

**Keep a copy.** Once you have your COE, save a digital copy. You'll potentially need it again if you use the VA loan benefit on a future transaction.

## Bottom Line

Getting your VA Certificate of Eligibility does not need to be a slow process. The electronic lender pull through Web LGY takes minutes for most veterans. VA.gov self-service works well for veterans with straightforward service histories who want to review their status independently. The mail-in route is a last resort.

For IRRRL borrowers specifically, the COE is usually handled entirely by the lender with no action required on your part.
