## The VA Has No Credit Score Minimum — Lenders Do

One of the most persistent misconceptions about VA loans is that the VA sets a minimum credit score. It does not. The Department of Veterans Affairs evaluates loan applications holistically — looking at residual income, debt-to-income ratios, credit history, and overall financial profile — without attaching a hard numerical floor to the credit score field.

This applies to the [VA Cash-Out Refinance](/va-cash-out) just as it does to VA purchase loans and the VA IRRRL.

What the VA does not do, however, virtually every approved lender does: set a minimum credit score as part of their own underwriting guidelines. These internal policies are called **overlays** — requirements that exceed the VA's own minimum standards. They exist because lenders bear real financial risk even on VA-guaranteed loans, and credit score is one of the most reliable predictors of default risk that lenders have available.

Understanding the difference between VA guidelines and lender overlays is the first step to navigating the credit score question accurately.

## Why Lenders Add Credit Score Overlays

The VA guaranty covers a portion of the loan if the veteran defaults, which reduces lender risk meaningfully — but does not eliminate it. For veterans with full entitlement, there is no VA-imposed loan limit, though lenders may set their own caps. The county conforming limit framework still applies for veterans with partial entitlement. Regardless of loan size, the lender still holds the unguaranteed portion and bears the cost of the foreclosure process, carrying costs during default, and any loss beyond what the VA covers.

Lenders also sell most VA loans into the secondary market — to investors who buy mortgage-backed securities. Those investors have their own risk tolerance and credit score expectations. A lender who originates too many loans to lower-score borrowers may find them harder to sell or may receive worse pricing on the secondary market.

The result: lenders set minimums that reflect their own risk appetite and their investors' requirements, independent of what the VA technically allows.

## Typical Lender Credit Score Tiers

Minimums vary significantly across lenders. The ranges below are approximate market observations — not industry standards — and individual lenders may fall outside them. The VA itself does not impose a minimum score on any of these programs, but lender overlays vary by product, and cash-out refinances often have stricter minimums than IRRRLs or purchase loans.

**620 minimum** — Some lenders, particularly those that specialize in VA lending and have investors willing to accept more credit risk, will approve cash-out refinances down to 620. These tend to be larger institutions with dedicated VA departments or non-bank lenders focused on government-backed loans. Expect fewer options and higher rates at this tier.

**640 minimum** — A common floor at many VA-approved lenders. Borrowers in the 640–679 range can typically find multiple lenders willing to approve a cash-out refinance, though rate pricing will reflect the additional risk.

**660–680 minimum** — Many lenders target this range as their standard minimum for cash-out transactions specifically (as opposed to purchase loans or IRRRLs, which may have slightly lower minimums at the same lender). Cash-out refinances carry more risk than rate-reduction refinances, and lender minimums often reflect that.

**720+ for best rates** — Approval and favorable pricing are different things. Veterans with scores above 720 — and especially above 740 — typically access the most competitive rate tiers. The difference between being approved at 640 and being approved at 740 can mean meaningfully different monthly payments over the life of the loan.

## Approval vs. a Good Deal: An Important Distinction

Getting approved is not the same as getting a good deal. This distinction matters significantly for VA Cash-Out Refinances, where the loan amounts are often large and the funding fee is already a substantial upfront cost.

Consider the rate impact of credit score on a $350,000 cash-out refinance:

| Credit Score | Approximate Rate | Monthly P&I | 30-Year Interest Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| 740+ | 6.25% | ~$2,155 | ~$426,000 |
| 680–739 | 6.75% | ~$2,270 | ~$467,000 |
| 620–679 | 7.25% | ~$2,389 | ~$510,000 |

These figures are illustrative — actual rates vary by lender, market conditions, loan-to-value ratio, and other factors. But the directional relationship is consistent: lower credit scores produce higher rates, and over 30 years the difference compounds into tens of thousands of dollars.

A veteran approved at 620 is not getting the same loan as a veteran approved at 740. They are getting access to the same program, at a higher cost.

## How the VA Cash-Out's Risk Profile Affects Credit Requirements

Lenders often apply stricter credit score minimums to cash-out refinances than to purchase loans or IRRRLs — and there are legitimate reasons for this.

A cash-out refinance increases the loan balance. The veteran leaves closing with more debt than they had before, secured by the same home. From a lender's perspective, a borrower who is stretching their finances to take cash out of their home represents a different risk profile than one who is simply buying or doing a rate-reduction refinance. The higher loan-to-value ratio that often accompanies cash-out transactions compounds this.

The result: if a lender approves IRRRLs at 580, they may require 640 for a cash-out. If they approve purchases at 620, they may require 660 for cash-out. Check with individual lenders — the gap between their IRRRL minimum and their cash-out minimum is often 20–40 points.

## Manual Underwriting: An Option for Some Borrowers

Some lenders will manually underwrite VA loan applications that fall below their standard automated minimums, provided the borrower has strong compensating factors. Manual underwriting means a human underwriter reviews the full file rather than relying solely on an automated approval system. Manual underwriting is more commonly available for VA purchase loans. For cash-out refinances, many lenders treat their published credit score minimum as a firm cutoff — it is worth asking, but don't count on it as a reliable path if your score is meaningfully below the lender's stated floor.

Compensating factors that can support a manual underwriting approval include:

- **High residual income** — the VA's residual income standard measures how much money is left after all monthly obligations. A veteran whose residual income significantly exceeds the VA's minimum for their region and family size presents a stronger profile even with a lower score.
- **Substantial reserves** — cash or liquid assets equal to 6+ months of mortgage payments demonstrate the ability to weather income disruption.
- **Stable long-term employment** — multi-year tenure with the same employer, particularly in a stable industry, signals lower default risk.
- **Low debt-to-income ratio** — a DTI well below the VA's 41% guideline leaves more margin in the borrower's budget.
- **Clean recent payment history** — a veteran with a 610 score due to old collections but an otherwise spotless payment record over the past 24 months looks different to a manual underwriter than one with recent late payments.

Not all lenders offer manual underwriting for cash-out refinances, and those that do will still have a floor below which they won't go regardless of compensating factors. But if you are 20–30 points below a lender's stated minimum and your overall financial picture is strong, it is worth asking whether manual underwriting is available.

## Improving Your Score Before Applying

If your credit score is below where you want it to be for a cash-out refinance, a targeted effort to raise it before applying can pay off significantly — both in access to more lenders and in the rate you ultimately receive.

The most effective steps:

**Pay down revolving balances.** Credit utilization — the ratio of your current balance to your credit limit on revolving accounts — has an outsized effect on your score. Getting balances below 30% of each card's limit, and ideally below 10%, can produce meaningful score improvements within 1–2 billing cycles.

**Don't open new credit accounts.** New inquiries and new accounts both create short-term score dips. In the months before applying for a VA Cash-Out Refinance, avoid opening new credit cards, auto loans, or other credit lines.

**Dispute errors on your credit report.** Incorrect derogatory marks — a collection that was paid off, an account not belonging to you, a late payment recorded in error — can be disputed with the credit bureaus. Successful disputes remove the negative item and can produce meaningful score gains. Pull your reports from annualcreditreport.com and review them before starting the refinance process.

**Let negative items age.** Derogatory items lose scoring impact over time. A collection from 5 years ago hurts less than one from 6 months ago. If you can delay your refinance by 6–12 months while negative items age and utilization comes down, the rate improvement may more than offset any change in the interest rate environment.

**Don't close old accounts.** Closing a credit card reduces your total available credit, which increases your utilization ratio and can shorten your average account age. Both effects typically lower your score.

## How Many Lenders to Shop

Given how significantly rates vary by credit score — and how differently lenders price lower-score borrowers — shopping multiple lenders is more important, not less, when your score is below 720. A borrower at 680 will find more rate variation across lenders than a borrower at 760, because lenders have more discretion in how they price credit risk in the middle tiers.

Get Loan Estimates from at least three lenders. Compare the APR, not just the stated rate, and pay attention to origination fees and discount points, which some lenders use to offset risk rather than adjusting the rate directly. The [VA Cash-Out Refinance](/va-cash-out) process is the same across lenders — the pricing is not.

## The Funding Fee and Your Score

Credit score does not affect the VA funding fee. Whether your score is 620 or 760, the funding fee on a first-use VA cash-out refinance is 2.15% of the loan amount; subsequent use is 3.3%. Veterans with a service-connected disability rating are exempt regardless of score. See the full [VA funding fee guide](/blog/va-funding-fee-2026) for rates, exemptions, and dollar examples.

What your score does affect is the interest rate — and that rate, compounded over the life of the loan, is almost always a larger financial variable than the one-time funding fee.

## Bottom Line

The VA sets no credit score floor for cash-out refinances, but every lender does — typically 620 to 680 as a minimum, with the best rates reserved for scores above 720. Getting approved and getting a competitive rate are separate questions, and the gap between them can mean tens of thousands of dollars over the life of the loan.

If your score needs work, focused effort on utilization and payment history can produce meaningful gains in a relatively short time. If your score is already solid, shop at least three lenders — the rate variation for VA Cash-Out Refinances is real, and the effort required to compare Loan Estimates is small relative to the savings available.
