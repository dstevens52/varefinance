## Two Paths to a Lower Rate

If you are a veteran with a VA loan looking to lower your interest rate, you have a choice most homeowners do not: refinance using the [VA IRRRL (Streamline Refinance)](/va-irrrl) or refinance conventionally through a standard rate-and-term refinance. Both get you to a lower rate. But they get there very differently — and the right choice depends on your specific situation.

This article breaks down the key differences so you can make an informed decision.

## What Is the VA IRRRL?

The VA Interest Rate Reduction Refinance Loan is a government-backed refinance product available exclusively to veterans and service members who already have a VA-backed mortgage. It is designed to be fast, simple, and low-cost. The VA has already guaranteed your current loan; the IRRRL simply updates the rate under that existing framework.

A conventional refinance, by contrast, treats you as a new borrower from scratch. The lender has no prior relationship with your loan and must fully re-underwrite you — verifying income, ordering an appraisal, reviewing your credit and assets — before agreeing to extend new terms.

## Appraisal: The Biggest Practical Difference

**VA IRRRL:** In most cases, no appraisal is required. The VA has pre-approved the property through your original loan. Unless the lender has specific concerns or is using automated valuation models that return unusual results, you can refinance without a formal in-person appraisal.

**Conventional refinance:** A full appraisal is almost always required. The appraiser visits the property, assesses condition and comparable sales, and produces a formal report. This typically costs $500–$900 and takes 1–3 weeks to schedule and complete.

Why does this matter beyond the cost? If your home has declined in value since you purchased it, a conventional refinance may be impossible — lenders generally cap loan-to-value ratios at 80–97% depending on the program. With an IRRRL, no appraisal means no LTV risk. Your home's current market value is irrelevant to the transaction.

## Income and Employment Verification

**VA IRRRL:** Most IRRRL transactions require no income verification. The VA's position is that if you have been making your current mortgage payments, you have demonstrated the ability to continue doing so. Most lenders will not ask for pay stubs, W-2s, or tax returns.

**Conventional refinance:** Full income documentation is standard. You will provide 2 years of W-2s or tax returns, recent pay stubs, and possibly a verification of employment. If you are self-employed, expect additional scrutiny — 2 years of business returns, a profit-and-loss statement, and possibly a CPA letter.

For veterans whose income situation has changed — a career transition, a period of self-employment, reduced hours — the IRRRL's minimal documentation requirement is a meaningful advantage.

## Closing Costs: Funding Fee vs. Conventional Costs

This is where the comparison gets nuanced.

**VA IRRRL closing costs:**
- VA funding fee: 0.5% of the loan amount
- Lender origination fee (if any)
- Title insurance and recording fees
- Total: typically 1–2% of the loan amount

**Conventional refinance closing costs:**
- No VA funding fee
- Lender origination fee
- Appraisal ($500–$900)
- Title insurance and recording fees
- Total: typically 2–3% of the loan amount

At first glance, the VA funding fee seems like a disadvantage. But consider: the IRRRL's 0.5% fee replaces both the appraisal cost and any private mortgage insurance. A conventional borrower with less than 20% equity will pay PMI monthly for years — often $100–$300 per month — while the VA borrower pays a one-time fee and never deals with PMI again.

**One critical exemption:** Veterans with a service-connected disability rating of 10% or higher are typically **exempt from the VA funding fee entirely**. If you have any disability rating, verify your exemption status before closing on any VA loan transaction. This exemption alone can eliminate the one cost category where conventional refinancing appears more competitive.

## Private Mortgage Insurance: A Major Long-Term Differentiator

VA loans never require PMI, regardless of how much equity you have. Conventional loans require it whenever the loan-to-value ratio exceeds 80%.

If you refinanced conventionally with less than 20% equity, you would be paying PMI until you either paid down the loan to 80% LTV or requested its cancellation. On a $350,000 loan, PMI typically runs $1,400–$5,250 per year. That cost compounds over years and can substantially erode the savings from a lower interest rate.

The IRRRL keeps you in VA-backed territory — and permanently out of PMI territory.

## Credit Score Requirements

**VA IRRRL:** The VA does not set a minimum credit score for the IRRRL. Individual lenders often impose overlays (typically 580–620 minimum), but the requirements are generally more lenient than conventional products.

**Conventional refinance:** Most conventional lenders require a minimum 620 credit score, with better rates reserved for scores above 740. If your credit has taken a hit since your original mortgage, a conventional refinance may offer less favorable terms than your current loan.

## When a Conventional Refinance Might Make More Sense

Despite the VA IRRRL's advantages, there are specific scenarios where conventional refinancing is worth considering.

**You want to remove VA loan entitlement from the property.** If you want to free up your VA entitlement for a future home purchase, a conventional refinance pays off the VA loan and releases the entitlement — something the IRRRL does not do.

**You have substantial equity and excellent credit.** Borrowers with 20%+ equity and credit scores above 760 can sometimes access competitive conventional rates with minimal lender fees, and the 0.5% VA funding fee (even small as it is) becomes a point of comparison.

**You want to add or remove a borrower.** Adding a spouse to the mortgage or removing a co-borrower generally requires a full refinance. The IRRRL has limited flexibility here — a non-veteran co-borrower can be removed via IRRRL, but adding one typically requires a full Cash-Out Refinance or conventional product.

## Side-by-Side Comparison

| | VA IRRRL | Conventional Refinance |
|---|---|---|
| Appraisal required | Usually not | Yes |
| Income verification | Usually not | Yes |
| PMI required | Never | If LTV > 80% |
| Funding fee | 0.5% (exempt if disabled) | None |
| Minimum credit score | ~580–620 (lender overlay) | 620+ |
| Closing timeline | 14–21 days | 30–45 days |
| Available to non-VA loan holders | No | Yes |

## The Bottom Line

For most veterans with existing VA loans who simply want a lower rate, the VA IRRRL wins on nearly every dimension: lower documentation burden, no appraisal risk, no PMI, faster closing, and more lenient credit requirements. The 0.5% funding fee is a modest one-time cost that is often outweighed by the appraisal savings alone — and disappears entirely for disabled veterans.

A conventional refinance has its place in specific circumstances — primarily when you want to exit the VA loan program, add a borrower, or have strategic reasons to release VA entitlement. But for a straightforward rate reduction, the IRRRL is almost always the better path for eligible veterans.
